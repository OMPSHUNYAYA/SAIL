# **SECTION D — Memoryless Certification**

## **D1. What does memoryless mean?**

Memoryless means certified correctness does not depend on hidden historical state.

The material required to reproduce the result remains available through visible structure, evidence, and certificates.

---

## **D2. Does SAIL support persistence?**

Yes.

SAIL supports persistence, operational records, export packs, and replay.

However, deterministic certificate correctness is not intended to depend on hidden memory.

---

## **D3. What replaces hidden memory as the basis of correctness?**

SAIL uses:

`Visible Input -> Evidence -> Certificate -> Replay`

The visible structural record carries the information required for verification.

---

## **D4. Why is replay important?**

Replay allows an independent reviewer to test whether the same certified structure produces the same result.

Replay invariant:

`same certified structure -> same replay result`

---

## **D5. What is the primary replay invariant?**

`same structure -> same evidence -> same certificate`

The broader release invariant is:

`same structure -> same evidence -> same certificate -> same replayable outcome`

---

## **D6. What would break the memoryless guarantee?**

The guarantee would be broken if:

* certificate generation required hidden prior-session state
* replay depended on inaccessible external context
* identical visible inputs produced different certificates because of hidden state
* correctness depended on information absent from the structural record

The guarantee is preserved when:

* certificate inputs come from visible structural fields
* the same structural payload produces the same certificate
* replay does not require hidden memory
* operational metadata does not alter structural identity

---

# **SECTION E — Structural and Operational Identity**

## **E1. What is structural identity?**

Structural identity describes what a structure **is**.

It is deterministically derived from structural content.

Invariant:

`same structural payload -> same structural identity`

---

## **E2. What is operational identity?**

Operational identity identifies a particular runtime record, event, or operational instance.

Examples include:

* record IDs
* creation timestamps
* update timestamps
* export timestamps
* delivery modes

---

## **E3. Are structural identity and operational identity the same?**

No.

`structural identity != operational record identity`

Two operational records may represent the same structural content while retaining separate record identities.

---

## **E4. Why separate structural and operational identity?**

The separation supports both:

* deterministic replay
* operational traceability

The structural certificate remains stable while operational systems can still track when, where, or how a record was created.

---

## **E5. Can timestamps change a structural certificate?**

They should not.

Operational timestamps are excluded from deterministic structural certificate generation.

Invariant:

`timestamp change -> no change to structural certificate`

---

## **E6. Can random identifiers influence certificates?**

They should not.

Random identifiers, operational record IDs, interface state, and export timestamps must not be included in structural certificate inputs.

---

# **SECTION F — Determinism and Replay**

## **F1. Is SAIL deterministic?**

SAIL is deterministic within the bounded reference implementation when certificate inputs are derived exclusively from visible structural fields.

Core invariants include:

`same structure -> same evidence`

`same structure -> same certificate`

`same structure -> same replay result`

`same structural payload -> same structural identity`

---

## **F2. Why is determinism important?**

Determinism supports:

* reproducibility
* certificate comparison
* auditability
* replayability
* independent verification
* falsification testing

---

## **F3. Can equivalent structures produce different certificates?**

They should not.

A reproducible case where equivalent structural inputs produce different certificates would falsify the deterministic guarantee being tested.

---

## **F4. What can invalidate deterministic behavior?**

Determinism may be invalidated when certificate inputs improperly include:

* random identifiers
* timestamps
* interface state
* hidden external state
* mutable global state
* operational record identity

---

## **F5. What is idempotency?**

Idempotency means repeating an already accepted lifecycle transition does not create a different structural result.

Invariant:

`repeat accepted transition -> same certificate + unchanged transition timestamp`

Examples include repeated:

* requirement approval
* requirement realization
* incident escalation
* incident recovery
* incident verification

---

## **F6. What happens when structural input changes?**

Certificates that depend on the changed input must be recomputed or cleared.

Reset invariant:

`changed upstream structure -> dependent downstream certificates reset`

This prevents certificates issued for an old structure from being reused for a changed structure.

---

# **SECTION G — Lifecycle Guards and Fail-Closed Behavior**

## **G1. What is a lifecycle guard?**

A lifecycle guard is a structural precondition controlling whether a downstream transition is allowed.

General invariant:

`downstream certification allowed iff required upstream structures are certified`

---

## **G2. What does fail closed mean?**

Fail closed means an incomplete, contradictory, unresolved, or unauthorized structural state does not silently become certified.

A blocked state remains visible.

---

## **G3. What are examples of guarded transitions?**

Examples include:

* approval blocked before trace certification
* requirement realization blocked before approval
* incident recovery blocked before required escalation
* incident verification blocked before recovery
* federation replay blocked before network resolution
* federation audit blocked before replay

---

## **G4. Does pressing a button guarantee certification?**

No.

A button or API call requests a transition.

The transition is accepted only when its structural preconditions are satisfied.

---

## **G5. Are blocked states part of validation?**

Yes.

A complete verification suite must test both:

* successful paths
* blocked paths

A happy-path demonstration alone is insufficient.

# **SECTION H — Coordination and Federation**

## **H1. What is Structural Coordination?**

Structural Coordination allows multiple structures to resolve and maintain a shared state through common invariants.

Core chain:

`Structure A + Structure B + Structure N -> Coordination Invariant -> Coordinated State`

---

## **H2. What is Structural Federation?**

Structural Federation enables decentralized certified coordination across multiple nodes or runtimes.

---

## **H3. Does federation require a central controller?**

Not as a structural requirement.

Federation is resolved through visible node structures, network configuration, quorum, consensus, replay, and audit conditions.

---

## **H4. What is the correct federation chain?**

`Node Identity -> Peer Exchange -> Quorum -> Consensus -> Federated Replay -> Federated Audit`

Quorum is resolved before consensus because consensus certification depends on sufficient certified participation.

---

## **H5. What is a quorum?**

A quorum is the minimum certified-node participation required by the network configuration.

Invariant:

`quorum certified iff certified node count >= configured quorum`

Quorum belongs to network configuration.

It is not part of an individual node's identity.

---

## **H6. What is consensus?**

Consensus represents certified agreement across participating nodes.

Consensus requires:

* quorum
* aligned network identity
* aligned scope

Invariant:

`consensus certified iff quorum met AND network identity aligned AND scope aligned`

---

## **H7. Are quorum and consensus the same?**

No.

Quorum is a participation-count condition.

Consensus is a structural-agreement condition.

A network may meet quorum while nodes disagree.

Nodes may agree while insufficient certified participation exists.

Both conditions are independently required.

---

## **H8. When is federation certified?**

`federation certified iff network resolved AND quorum certified AND consensus certified`

---

## **H9. When is federated replay allowed?**

`federated replay allowed iff federation certified`

---

## **H10. When is federated audit allowed?**

`federated audit allowed iff federation certified AND replay certified`

---

## **H11. Do pending nodes count toward certified quorum?**

No.

Pending or uncertified nodes do not satisfy certified quorum.

---

## **H12. Can a network mismatch still produce consensus?**

No.

Network identity mismatch or scope mismatch must prevent consensus certification.

---
